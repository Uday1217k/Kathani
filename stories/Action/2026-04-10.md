# The Parrot's Secret

**Genre:** Action | **Date:** 2026-04-10

### Characters
*   **Maya:** A smart, observant young woman who works as a pet sitter.
*   **Polly:** A grey parrot, Mr. Henderson's pet.
*   **Mr. Henderson:** Polly's owner, currently away.
*   **"Mr. Finch":** An intruder posing as an official.

### The Story
Maya hummed as she wiped down the counter. Pet sitting for Mr. Henderson was usually easy. The old house was quiet, and Polly, the grey parrot, mostly sat on her perch, munching seeds. Mr. Henderson had been very specific about Polly’s care: fresh water twice a day, special seed mix, and always, *always* make sure the living room window was locked. Maya had followed the rules for three days now.

She was just about to give Polly her afternoon seeds when the doorbell rang. Maya checked the peephole. A man stood there, clean-cut, wearing a uniform. He held a clipboard. "Animal Control," he said loudly through the door. "We've had reports of unusual bird activity in the area."

Maya frowned. "Unusual bird activity? What does that mean?"

"Just a routine check, ma'am. We've had a few cases of a rare feather disease spreading. Need to check local birds, make sure they're healthy." He smiled, but his eyes were too sharp. Maya knew Mr. Henderson’s house was a fortress of quiet. No neighbours had exotic birds. This felt off.

"I’m sorry," Maya said, "Mr. Henderson isn't home. I'm just watching his parrot. She's fine."

"Can't be too careful," the man insisted, still smiling. "Just a quick look, through the window even. Don't want any trouble."

Maya felt a chill. "I don't think that's a good idea. Mr. Henderson is very private." She didn't like the way he was trying to see past her, straining to catch a glimpse of the living room. Or maybe, of Polly.

The man’s smile dropped a little. "Look, lady, I can come back with a warrant if you want to make this harder."

"I think I'll just call Mr. Henderson," Maya replied, grabbing her phone. The man's eyes widened slightly. He mumbled something, then turned and walked quickly down the path. Maya watched him get into a plain white van, not an official Animal Control vehicle, and speed away.

Her heart thumped. Something was very wrong. She went through the house, checking every lock, every window. All secure. She picked up Polly and held her close. "Good bird," she whispered. Polly ruffled her feathers and let out a string of squawks. "Pretty Polly, pretty Polly!" she chirped, then added a series of quick, sharp noises, almost like a code. It sounded like "Click-buzz-whir-zip!"

Maya put Polly back on her perch. The old house felt bigger, darker. She decided to stay in the living room, near Polly. She kept an eye on the front door. About an hour later, she heard a faint scraping sound from the back of the house.

Someone was trying to get in.

Maya grabbed the heavy iron poker from beside the fireplace. Her mind raced. She couldn't call the police right away, not without proof. She had to think fast. She remembered Mr. Henderson’s very strict rules about Polly's schedule. He always insisted on a specific type of classical music playing softly when she was given her dinner. "It helps her digest," he'd said with a wink.

Maya didn’t think digestion was the real reason.

The scraping stopped. She heard a soft creak. Someone was inside the back door. Maya held her breath. The intruder was moving slowly, quietly, through the kitchen. He was careful, but not careful enough. Maya knew the house better than he did. She knew about the creaky floorboard near the pantry.

*Creaaaak.*

He was getting closer to the living room. Maya had an idea. She knew the back gate was unlocked. She also knew that Mr. Henderson had a small, very loud siren hidden in the garden shed, meant to scare off raccoons. She had seen him test it once; it was ear-splitting.

She made a decision. She carefully, silently, set the heavy poker against the living room door frame, ready to jam it if the door opened. Then, she opened the living room window just enough to slide out quickly. She ran to the garden shed, fumbling for the siren switch.

As the intruder reached the living room, he pushed the door gently. It opened a crack. He peered in. No Maya. His eyes went straight to Polly. He took a step inside.

That was Maya’s cue. She flipped the switch. The siren blared, a piercing, deafening shriek that echoed through the quiet house.

The man yelled in surprise, clutching his ears. Polly squawked frantically, flapping her wings. The man stumbled back, bumping into a small table. He didn't try to stop, didn't try to look for Maya. He just ran, scrambling back the way he came, out the back door, and over the fence.

Maya turned off the siren. Silence fell, broken only by Polly’s nervous chirps. Maya walked back into the living room, poker still in hand. She looked at Polly, who had settled down, blinking at her.

"Pretty Polly," Polly squawked again, then repeated the strange sequence: "Click-buzz-whir-zip!"

### Conclusion
Maya looked at the small, plain, wooden box near Polly's perch, the one Mr. Henderson had said was Polly's "favorite toy," and that she should always ensure was "well-hidden but accessible." It was just a simple puzzle box with sliding wooden pieces. Nobody would ever guess it wasn't a toy. Maya had always thought it was just a strange thing for an old man to be so specific about. Now, the man in the uniform had only been interested in Polly, not money, not jewels. And Polly kept repeating that odd set of sounds. It wasn't just noise. Maya suddenly understood. Polly wasn't just a pet. She was a talking vault, a living message. And that wooden box wasn't a toy; it was the key, a simple decoder for Polly's secret language. Mr. Henderson hadn't hired a pet sitter; he'd hired a bodyguard, unknowingly, for his feathered code machine.
