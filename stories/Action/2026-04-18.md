# The Starter's Run

**Genre:** Action | **Date:** 2026-04-18

### Characters
Leo: A quick and clever bicycle courier.
Chef Antoine: A famous and intense baker.
The Men: Two shadowy figures trying to stop Leo.

### The Story
The call came in fast. A special delivery, top priority. "The Crust & Crumb bakery to the Grand Imperial Hotel. For Chef Antoine, specifically," the voice on the phone said. The pay was triple the usual rate. Leo, a bike courier who knew every shortcut and alley in the city, took the job. He needed the cash.

At the bakery, an old woman with flour on her apron handed him a small, plain white box. "Handle this with care, young man," she warned, her eyes serious. "It's more precious than gold." Leo nodded, used to people thinking their parcels were the most important thing in the world. This box felt light, almost empty.

He strapped the box onto his bike and pushed off. The city hummed around him. He pedaled through the morning rush, weaving past cars and buses. Suddenly, a black van swerved hard, cutting him off. Leo slammed his brakes, narrowly avoiding a crash. The van sped past, then slowed down a block ahead.

A shiver went down Leo's spine. This wasn't a normal traffic move. He looked in his mirror. A dark motorcycle pulled out from behind the van, the rider wearing a black helmet. They were coming for him.

Leo pedaled harder, ducking into a narrow alley he knew well. He bumped over cracked pavement, hearing the roar of the motorcycle trying to follow. The alley was too tight for the van. He came out onto a busy market street, dodging fruit stands and pedestrians. The motorcycle was still behind him, now weaving through people.

He took a sharp turn into a park. The motorcycle wouldn't be able to keep up on the winding paths. He zipped past joggers and dog walkers, hearing angry shouts from the motorcycle rider as he struggled on the grass. Leo didn't look back. He just focused on the hotel.

He burst out of the park and onto the main road leading to the Grand Imperial. The van was waiting, blocking the hotel's front entrance. Leo had to think fast. He spotted the service entrance, usually for deliveries. He made a sharp turn, racing down the ramp. The van started to follow but got stuck trying to make the tight turn.

Leo skidded to a halt inside the hotel's bustling kitchen. Chef Antoine, a man with a stern face and flour-dusted uniform, was waiting, tapping his foot. "You're late!" he barked, but his eyes were wide with relief.

Leo unstrapped the box, heart still pounding. "Had a little trouble," he gasped, handing it over.

### Conclusion
Chef Antoine snatched the box and tore it open. Inside, nestled in bubble wrap, was a small glass jar. It held a pale, bubbly, sticky goo. "Boris the Third!" Chef Antoine cried, his voice full of relief. "You saved him!" Leo stared, confused. "Boris?" he asked. Chef Antoine gently picked up the jar. "My sourdough starter! It's over a hundred years old, perfect for my competition bread tonight. Someone tried to steal it to ruin me. It's irreplaceable, you understand? Thank you, young man. You delivered gold." Leo looked at the jar of bubbly paste, then at the frantic chef, and finally understood why a simple box could be worth so much trouble.
