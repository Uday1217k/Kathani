# The Coffee Run

**Genre:** Action | **Date:** 2026-06-13

### Characters
*   Leo: A quick and clever bike courier.
*   Mr. Thorne: A rich man with very specific tastes.
*   The 'Cleaners': Mr. Thorne's security team, dressed as bad guys.

### The Story
Leo checked his watch. The sun was hot on his neck. His new job was simple: pick up a small black box from a lab and take it to a fancy office tower across town. The pay was huge, much more than normal for a simple delivery. The client, a Mr. Thorne, said the item was "urgent and vital." Leo felt a shiver. He pictured secret plans or a rare jewel. This was big.

He got the box. It was black, cold, and fit in his hand. No labels. Just a heavy feeling. He tucked it carefully into his special insulated bag. As he rode his bike out of the alley, a dark sedan pulled away from the curb. It went too fast. Too close. Leo felt his gut twist.

He took a sharp turn onto a busy street. The sedan followed. Leo weaved his bike between taxis and buses, fast and smooth. He knew the city like the back of his hand. He cut down a narrow side lane, scattering pigeons. The sedan tried to follow, honking loudly, but got stuck behind a slow delivery truck. Leo smiled. Not bad for a first try.

But then, a black van appeared from another street, cutting him off. Two big men in dark clothes jumped out. Leo slammed his brakes. He turned his bike sharply, kicking over a stack of empty crates to block their path. He pedaled hard, pushing his legs. The men shouted, but he was already gone, racing through a park, dodging people walking their dogs.

This was crazy. This wasn't a normal delivery. He was being hunted. The package must be something huge. He imagined headlines: "Brave Courier Saves Day!" He felt a surge of energy. He was good at this. He had to get this box to Mr. Thorne.

He used all his tricks. He rode through a busy market, making fruit carts wobble. He ducked into a subway station, dragging his bike down the stairs. He rode it through the empty platform tunnels between trains. He knew all the shortcuts, the secret ways. The chasers were always a step behind, but they were persistent. He heard sirens sometimes, far away, probably for the mess he was making.

Finally, panting and sweating, he reached the skyscraper. He saw two dark cars waiting at the main entrance. "No way," he muttered. He swerved, aiming for the back alley. He saw a service entrance, a small door. He jammed his foot on the metal push bar. It opened. He burst inside.

He ran through a clean, white hallway, ignoring a confused cleaner. He found the elevator, pushed the top button, and held the box tight. His chest hurt from all the running. The elevator doors opened on the penthouse floor. A woman in a sharp suit stood waiting. She looked calm, too calm.

"Mr. Thorne is waiting, Leo," she said, her voice smooth. She led him into a huge office. Glass walls showed the whole city spread out below. A man, Mr. Thorne, stood by a big desk. He was older, with kind eyes, but a firm jaw.

Leo was still breathing heavy. He put the black box on the desk. "Here, sir," he said, trying to sound tough. "It wasn't easy."

Mr. Thorne picked up the box. He turned it over in his hand, a small smile playing on his lips. "I imagine not, Leo. You did exceptionally well."

Then he pressed a hidden button on the box. A small lid popped open. Inside, nestled on a soft cushion, was a single, perfect espresso coffee pod.

Leo blinked. "A... coffee pod?"

### Conclusion
Mr. Thorne chuckled. "Not just any coffee pod, Leo. This is the last remaining pod of 'Ethiopian Sunrise,' a very rare blend. My personal coffee machine only takes these exact pods, and it broke down this morning. My usual supplier is closed for renovation. I needed it urgently." He took a deep breath. "And the chase? That was my security team, the 'Cleaners.' I needed to know if my courier could handle pressure. You passed, Leo. You start full-time tomorrow. My mornings are... very important." Leo just stood there, staring at the coffee pod, the city lights twinkling behind Mr. Thorne. He had faced down a whole city for a single cup of coffee.
