# The Perpetual Parcel

**Genre:** Horror | **Date:** 2026-05-26

### Characters
Arthur: A quiet, ordinary man.
The Delivery Person: A young woman with a tired face, appears briefly.

### The Story
Arthur lived a quiet life. His days were calm, his nights peaceful. One Tuesday morning, a brown parcel sat on his doorstep. No sender's address, just his name printed neatly on a plain white label. He opened it. Inside, nestled in shredded paper, was a small, wooden bird. Carved from light wood, with simple, smooth lines. It was nice, in a plain way. He placed it on his mantelpiece.

The next day, Wednesday, another parcel. Same brown box, same label. Arthur felt a flicker of surprise. He opened it. Another wooden bird. Identical to the first. He shrugged. Maybe a mix-up? He put it beside the first one.

Thursday. Another box. Another bird. This time, Arthur felt a prickle of unease. Three identical wooden birds lined up on his mantelpiece. He looked out his window. The same delivery person, a young woman with a tired face, was already driving away. He hadn't heard the knock.

Friday. A fourth bird. Arthur didn't even bother to open it inside. He left the box by the door. This was getting ridiculous. He tried calling the postal service. They couldn't help without a sender's address or a tracking number. He couldn't return something that technically didn't come from anywhere.

The weekend came and went, bringing two more birds. Arthur now had six. He stacked the unopened boxes in his spare room. The mantelpiece was getting crowded. He felt a cold knot in his stomach. Who was sending these? Why?

He tried to stop them. He left a note for the delivery person: "NO MORE PACKAGES." He even stayed home, peeking through the curtains. On Monday, he saw the postal van stop. The delivery person walked to his door, placed a box down, and left without looking at the note. She just dropped it and went. Like she was programmed.

Arthur started throwing them away. He walked to the public bins, dumping the boxes deep inside. He drove them to the dump. He even burned a few in his fire pit, watching the little wooden birds curl and blacken. But every single morning, a new brown parcel waited. Always the same size. Always the same weight. Always the same bird inside.

His house began to fill. The spare room was overflowing. The living room had stacks in corners. He started to feel buried alive by these silent, wooden messengers. He couldn't sleep. He couldn't eat. His quiet life was now a loud, absurd nightmare of endless packages.

One morning, sitting among the towering stacks, Arthur picked up a newly delivered bird. He stared at it. It was identical to the thousands before it. Or was it? He looked closer. He picked up an older bird from the mantelpiece. He compared them. His eyes, tired from lack of sleep, struggled to find a difference.

He looked at another, and another. He lined up ten of them on his coffee table. He stared. For a long time. Then, very, very slowly, a chill that had nothing to do with the winter air seeped into his bones.

### Conclusion
He started checking them all, one by one. The birds he'd received a month ago were perfect. The ones from two weeks ago had tiny, faint lines. The ones from yesterday and today had clear, deep grooves. The lines weren't random. They formed a pattern. A pattern that slowly, day by day, bird by bird, became clearer. It was a single, growing crack that seemed to snake its way across the wooden surface, moving from the tail towards the head, like a slow, deliberate fracture. He picked up the bird that had arrived that very morning. The crack had reached the bird's small, smooth head. And as he held it, he felt a sharp, splintering pain start behind his own eyes.
