# The Heirloom Shed

**Genre:** Horror | **Date:** 2026-06-16

### Characters
*   Mark: A young man, early twenties, recently moved into his grandmother's old house.
*   Sarah: Mark's girlfriend, supportive and practical.
*   Grandma Elsie (mentioned): Mark's recently deceased grandmother, described as private and a talented carver.

### The Story
Mark felt a bit lost in Grandma Elsie's old house. It was bigger than he remembered, full of dark wood and quiet corners. He'd moved in after she passed, needing a cheap place and a fresh start. But the house felt less like a new beginning and more like a long, drawn-out goodbye.

He’d been clearing out the garage when he first saw it – a small shed tucked away at the back of the garden. It was old, leaning heavily to one side, painted a faded green. A heavy chain wrapped around the door, secured with a thick, rusty padlock. Grandma Elsie had always been private, but a locked shed felt like more than just privacy. It felt like a secret.

For weeks, Mark tried to ignore it. He mowed the lawn around it, trimmed the bushes near it. But his eyes kept drifting to the padlock, a dark, silent challenge. Sarah, his girlfriend, visited one Saturday. She saw him staring at it.
"What's in there?" she asked, kicking at a loose piece of wood near its base.
"No idea," Mark said. "Grandma never mentioned it. Nobody in the family seems to know either."
"Well, you've got the key to the house now, maybe a key for the shed too?"
Mark had looked through all of Grandma Elsie's old boxes, her hidden trinket drawers. No key for the shed. "Nope. Nothing."
"Then break it open," Sarah said simply. "It's your house now."

That was easier said than done. The padlock was a beast. He bought bolt cutters, the biggest ones he could find. The metal groaned, then snapped with a loud crack that echoed in the quiet garden. Mark pulled the chain away, his heart thumping. The door creaked open, just a crack, letting out a smell of damp earth and old dust.

He pushed it wider. The shed was surprisingly clean inside, not dusty like he thought. No cobwebs. A single light bulb hung from the ceiling, connected to an extension cord he hadn’t noticed snaking from the house. He flipped a switch near the door. The bulb flickered to life, casting a dim, yellow glow.

The shed was lined with shelves, each one carefully built. They were mostly empty, but each space had a small, neat label underneath it, written in Grandma Elsie’s familiar, looping handwriting.

Mark squinted, reading the first one. "Mark, Age 1."
He moved to the next. "Mark, Age 5."
Then, "Mark, Age 10."
"Mark, Age 16."
"Mark, Age 20."

Mark felt a strange chill. These labels were for *him*, at different points in his life. He looked closer. On the shelf labelled "Mark, Age 5," there was a small, crudely carved wooden figure. It was a boy, about five years old, with slightly too-large ears and a mop of curly hair. It looked vaguely like Mark did in old photos. The wood was old, dark, and a small crack ran down its back. On the "Mark, Age 10" shelf, another, slightly more refined carving stood, but it was missing an arm. He found several more, each one a different age, each one showing slight damage or imperfection. It was like a display of him, slowly aging, but each piece was flawed.

His grandmother had always been a talented woodcarver, but she usually made birds or flowers, never people. And definitely not *him*.

He walked around the tiny shed, his stomach tightening. Every shelf, every space, was labelled with an age from *his* life. And on a small workbench in the corner, he saw it. A fresh, untouched block of wood. Beside it, a set of sharp carving tools, meticulously laid out. And next to the tools, a recent photograph of Mark, smiling at his college graduation.

Underneath the photograph lay a small, old diary. Grandma Elsie's. He opened it to the last entry, dated just days before she passed.

The elegant script was shaky now. *“Almost ready for the last piece. He's growing so fast. This one will be perfect. No more mistakes. He will finally be complete.”*

Mark closed the diary, his breath catching in his throat. He looked at the empty shelf, labelled "Mark, Age 22" – his current age. He looked at the fresh block of wood, the tools, the photo of himself. He looked at the line of flawed, damaged wooden figures of his past self.

He wasn't collecting his milestones. She was collecting *him*. Piece by piece.

He heard Sarah’s voice calling his name from the house, asking if he’d found anything. He didn't answer. He just stood there, staring at the empty space meant for him, now, and at the tools waiting to shape the final, perfect version.

### Conclusion
Mark was the last, unfinished piece. He realized the shed wasn't a place for keeping secrets, but for *making* them. And he was the greatest secret of all, slowly, painstakingly, being carved into existence. What would happen when he was finally complete, "perfect," with "no more mistakes"? The thought made his blood run cold.
