# The Spotless Guest

**Genre:** Horror | **Date:** 2026-06-23

### Characters
Sarah: A young woman, new to the city, slightly messy.
Mrs. Finch: The unseen previous tenant, whose powerful will for order lives on.

### The Story
Sarah found the apartment by sheer luck. It was perfect. High ceilings, big windows, and already spotless. Not a speck of dust anywhere. She moved in with her boxes, happy to start fresh in the city.

In the first week, little things happened. Sarah would leave her keys on the counter, but find them later on the small hook by the door. She’d forget a dirty coffee mug in the sink, only to discover it washed and dried in the cupboard. She just smiled. "Guess I'm tidier than I thought," she mumbled, half-joking to herself. The apartment felt good, like it was helping her.

Then it got stranger. She'd put her books on the bedside table, but wake up to find them neatly lined up on the shelf across the room. Her worn-out comfy blanket, which she loved to leave draped over the sofa, would always be folded into a perfect square on the armrest. It wasn't just tidying; it was *rearranging*.

Sarah started to feel watched. She bought a small camera and set it up in the living room, pointing at the sofa. She left a pile of clothes on the floor, a half-eaten snack on the coffee table. When she checked the camera footage later, nothing. No one came in. But when she walked into the living room, her clothes were folded in her laundry basket, and the snack was gone. The coffee table gleamed.

A cold feeling settled in her stomach. This wasn't helpful anymore. This was creepy. The apartment wasn't just neat; it was demanding. It had its own rules. She tried to fight back. She deliberately pulled books off shelves, scattering them. She left her shoes in the middle of the hallway. She even spilled a few drops of water on the kitchen floor and didn't wipe them up.

She went out for a walk, feeling a childish defiance. When she came back, the apartment was pristine. The books were back on the shelves, perfectly straight. Her shoes were paired up in the closet. The kitchen floor was dry and shining. Even her toothbrush, which she usually left lying flat, stood upright in its holder.

Sarah felt a chill that had nothing to do with the outside air. She wasn't living here; she was being *managed*. Every time she tried to make her own space, the apartment pushed back, stronger. It felt like a gentle, unyielding hand guiding her, correcting her. It was always neat, always perfect, and always watching. She couldn't relax. She couldn't be herself.

She decided to leave. This wasn't a home; it was a prison of perfection.

### Conclusion
Sarah started packing her boxes. She pulled open the small, built-in cupboard above the sink, planning to take down the few mugs she’d bought. Behind it, almost hidden by peeling paint, she saw a small, metal plaque. It read: "In loving memory of Eleanor Finch. A life of grace and unparalleled order. May her spirit keep this home ever immaculate." Sarah shivered. She went to grab a box she'd just filled with her books. As her fingers touched the cardboard, she saw a spine-chilling thing. One by one, as if by an invisible hand, the books began to slide out of the box and return to their places on the living room shelf, settling with soft, precise thuds. Her packed clothes, in another box, were slowly unfolding themselves, each item neatly put back into her open closet. The apartment wasn't going to let her leave. It had found its new occupant, and she would simply have to learn to be perfect.
