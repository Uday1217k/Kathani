# The Lumen Lament

**Genre:** Comedy | **Date:** 2026-05-31

### Characters
Arthur Pringle: A man who likes to save money and solve problems, often in the wrong way.
Brenda Pringle: Arthur's patient wife, who enjoys seeing things clearly.

### The Story
Arthur Pringle stared at the electricity bill. It was a few pounds higher than last month. "Brenda," he announced, holding the paper like a crime scene clue, "this is unacceptable. We're wasting power. And I know exactly why." Brenda, stirring her tea, gave him a tired look. "Is it the fridge, Arthur? Or perhaps your five-hour documentary binges?" Arthur shook his head with grave certainty. "No. It's the kitchen light. It's too bright. All that unnecessary light, just eating up watts."

The very next day, Arthur came home with a tiny package. Inside was a light bulb advertised as 'Ultra-Efficient, Eye-Soothing Glow.' It looked like a small, frosted mushroom. He proudly screwed it into the kitchen ceiling fixture, replacing their perfectly good, bright bulb. When he flipped the switch, the kitchen dimmed to a moody twilight. Brenda squinted. "Arthur, I can barely see my hand in front of my face. Did you buy a nightlight?" Arthur beamed. "It's not about seeing things, Brenda, it's about *saving* things! The planet, our money… you'll adjust."

Adjusting was hard. Cooking dinner became a game of blind man's bluff. Brenda cooked by the glow of her phone flashlight, often mistaking salt for sugar. Arthur, ever the optimist, tried to make it work. He started wearing a small headlamp at the dinner table. When Brenda complained, he said, "It's like camping, isn't it? Builds character." He tripped over the vacuum cleaner twice and once poured orange juice into his coffee mug.

Determined to prove his point without admitting defeat, Arthur went further. "We need to make better use of the available light," he declared. He painted the kitchen walls a stark, shiny white, hoping they would bounce the meager glow around. They didn't. Then he bought a giant mirror and leaned it against one wall, thinking it would double the light. It only doubled the darkness. Brenda started doing all her food prep in the hallway. "Arthur, I can't even tell if these carrots are clean or just muddy," she grumbled, waving a vegetable vaguely in the direction of the mirror.

One evening, Arthur was trying to make toast, guiding the bread by the faint glimmer from his headlamp. He bumped into the counter, sending a cascade of spoons clattering to the floor. "That's it!" Brenda yelled, dropping her phone with a thud. "I cannot live in this cave anymore! My eyes hurt from squinting, my shins are bruised, and I just bit into a raw potato because I thought it was an apple!"

### Conclusion
As Brenda bent down to pick up her phone, which had landed face-down near the kitchen door, she noticed a small, steady, bright red light glowing on the side of their old TV remote. Arthur had a habit of leaving it on the kitchen table after watching his late-night shows. "Arthur," she said, her voice surprisingly calm, "what is this little red light?" Arthur looked down, then away, then back, sheepishly. "Oh, that's the new remote. It has a 'find me' function, so it's always on. Never thought about it much." Brenda picked up her phone, then pointed it at the wall near the table, illuminating not only the little red light on the remote, but also the small, dark box plugged into the back of the TV. "And that, Arthur? That blinking blue light? That's the external hard drive you leave running all night, buffering movies you never watch, because you can't be bothered to turn it off at the wall." Arthur's mouth opened, then closed. The kitchen light bulb, which had caused so much dim misery, had cost a few pennies. The remote's glowing beacon and the constantly whirring hard drive, however, had been quietly sucking up power for months. "Right," Brenda said, switching on her phone's brightest setting and finally seeing the mess on the floor. "Now, where's the *actual* light bulb?"
