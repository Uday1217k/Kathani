# The Zen of Instant Coffee

**Genre:** Comedy | **Date:** 2026-05-24

### Characters
Bob: A friendly but easily flustered man in his late thirties.
Ms. Peterson: Bob's new neighbor, a calm and practical woman in her fifties.

### The Story
Bob’s new neighbor, Ms. Peterson, had just moved in next door. He wanted to make a good first impression. Not just a wave, but a proper, "Welcome to the street!" kind of welcome. And what says welcome better than a freshly brewed cup of coffee? Bob decided coffee was the way to go.

He remembered a magazine article he’d read called "The Zen of Coffee: A Journey to the Perfect Brew." It promised coffee so good it would change your life. This was it. He would make Ms. Peterson the best coffee she’d ever had.

First, the article said, you must grind your beans by hand. An electric grinder, it claimed, "rips the soul from the bean." Bob didn't have a special hand grinder, so he found his old mortar and pestle. He put the beans in and started to bash them. It was much harder than it looked. Bits of coffee flew everywhere. Soon, his counter was dusty with dark speckles, and his arms ached. The grinding took nearly fifteen minutes to get a rough, uneven powder.

Next, the water. "Heat spring water to precisely 92 degrees Celsius," the article instructed. Bob found his old candy thermometer. He filled a small pot with bottled water and watched the mercury rise. It was either too hot or too cold. He kept taking the pot off the stove and putting it back on. This went on for ten minutes. Steam made the kitchen window foggy.

Then came the "blooming" phase. You had to pour just a little hot water over the grounds and let them sit for exactly thirty seconds. Bob poured, checked his watch, and stared. The grounds bubbled a little. He felt like he was performing a magic trick.

After that, the article detailed a slow, steady pour-over method using a French press, but only half-plunged. You had to stir, wait, then pour more water in a slow spiral. Bob's hands shook. He spilled a little hot water on his hand. "Ow!" he yelped, nearly dropping the whole thing. The kitchen counter was now a sticky mess of coffee grounds and spilled water. The smell of strong coffee filled the air. Bob was sweating.

Finally, after what felt like an hour, he had two cups of coffee ready. One for him, one for Ms. Peterson. He wiped his brow with the back of his hand, leaving a coffee smudge on his forehead. The doorbell rang. Perfect timing. He quickly tried to clear a spot on the counter, but it was useless. The kitchen looked like a coffee bean explosion.

Ms. Peterson stood at the door, a friendly smile on her face. "Hello, Bob! I just wanted to introduce myself properly."

"Oh, hi, Ms. Peterson! Please, come in!" Bob said, trying to sound calm, gesturing wildly towards the disaster zone that was his kitchen. "I just brewed some coffee. Thought you might like a cup."

Ms. Peterson stepped inside, her eyes widening slightly at the chaos. "Oh, my. You certainly did brew some coffee." She looked at the smudges on Bob’s face and the scattered grounds.

Bob proudly presented a steaming mug. "It's a special blend. 'The Zen of Coffee,' they call it."

### Conclusion
Ms. Peterson took the mug carefully. She brought it to her lips and took a small sip. Her polite smile stayed fixed, but her eyes crinkled at the corners. She cleared her throat gently. "Bob," she said, handing the cup back to him, "this smells absolutely wonderful, and I really appreciate the effort. But I actually only drink decaf. And usually, just instant."
