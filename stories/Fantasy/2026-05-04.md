# The Chirping Stones

**Genre:** Fantasy | **Date:** 2026-05-04

### Characters
Lyra: A young woman who makes and sells baskets.
Old Man Hemlock: The oldest man in the village, respected for his knowledge of the stones.
Other Villagers: People who live in Oakhaven.

### The Story
The village of Oakhaven sat by a clear, fast stream. Everyone in Oakhaven knew about the chirping stones. These were smooth, round rocks found in the stream bed. If you held one just right, very still, you could hear a faint, high-pitched sound, like a tiny bird hidden inside. Villagers believed the stones chirped about things that were going to happen.

Lyra made baskets. She was good at it, her fingers quick and clever. But she was not so clever about her cart. Its right wheel always made a loud, scraping squeak that hurt her ears. She had tried oil, she had tried tying rags, but nothing worked. The squeak stayed. Lyra didn't believe much in the chirping stones. She thought people just heard what they wanted to hear.

"My stone chirped about a sunny day," said Mrs. Gable, smiling as the sun shone bright. "And look!"
"Mine told me I'd find a dropped coin," said young Finn, holding up a shiny penny. "And here it is!"
Old Man Hemlock, the oldest in the village, was the best at hearing the stones. "My stone chirped about a very good fishing spot," he told Lyra one morning. "I am off to catch a big trout."

Lyra sighed. Her cart squeaked as she pulled it along. Maybe she should try a stone, just once. She found a smooth, grey one in the stream. She held it tight, her ear pressed close. She waited. A faint, quick chirp. It chirped about a patch of rare blue moss, far up the winding path to the old oak tree. Lyra knew that path. She always walked it. She had never seen blue moss there. "Silly stone," she mumbled.

Later that day, she walked the path. Her cart still squeaked. She looked for the blue moss, just because the stone had chirped. And there it was! Hidden under a thick bush, a small patch of moss the color of a summer sky. She usually walked right past that bush. "Well," she thought, "that was lucky, I guess."

Days went by. Her cart still squeaked. Old Man Hemlock came back from fishing, grumbling. "The stone chirped about the fishing spot, yes. But it did not chirp that the fish would be too clever for my net!" He showed her his empty basket.

Lyra stopped, her hands on her hips. Her cart gave a particularly loud, angry squeak. "What if," she thought, "the stones don't tell you what *will* happen, but what you *don't know* or *don't see*?"
The blue moss: she hadn't seen it before, but it had always been there. The stone had told her to look closer.
Old Man Hemlock's fish: the stone had told him where to go, but not that the fish would be smart. It had chirped about what he *didn't know* about that spot.

Lyra went back to the stream. She picked up another stone, a small, smooth one. She closed her eyes. She thought about her squeaky cart wheel. "Chirp," she whispered to the stone, "tell me about my cart."
The stone chirped. Not about the wheel being fixed. Not about a quiet ride. It chirped about a small, loose peg, hidden deep inside the axle, right where she always just put oil on the outside. A peg she had never seen, never thought to look for. A tiny, rusty pin that was about to come completely loose.

### Conclusion
Lyra hurried home. She took apart her cart wheel and found the tiny, rusty peg, just as the stone had chirped. It was almost out. She took it out, cleaned it, and hammered it back in tight. When she put the wheel back, the cart made no sound. Not a single squeak. The stones didn't tell the future. They chirped about what you were missing right now, what you needed to notice, or what was about to cause trouble if you didn't pay attention. They whispered the present, so you could fix your future yourself.
