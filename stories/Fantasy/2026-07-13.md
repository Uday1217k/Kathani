# The Kindness Clouds

**Genre:** Fantasy | **Date:** 2026-07-13

### Characters
Elara: A young woman with a constant dull, grey cloud above her head.
Old Traveler: A kind, wise woman with a soft white cloud.
A Small Boy: Who scrapes his knee.

### The Story
Elara walked with a slouch. Above her head, a small, dark grey cloud floated. It didn't rain much, just a soft, steady drizzle. Everyone had one of these clouds. They floated about a foot above each person’s head, moving when they moved, staying put when they stopped. People said the clouds showed what was inside you. Bright yellow meant happy. Dark, stormy grey meant angry or sad. Elara’s cloud was just… dull. It had been dull for as long as she could remember.

"Looks like a drizzle day for you again, Elara," said Mr. Gable from his bakery door. His own cloud was a puffy white, with hints of blue. He always smiled. Elara nodded, trying to force a bright smile. She wanted her cloud to be sunny. She wanted it to be like the cheerful clouds of the village children, or the warm orange cloud of the town’s storyteller.

Elara tried everything. She sang happy songs, even when she didn't feel like it. She told herself she was happy, over and over. She even tried doing good deeds, like helping Mrs. Periwinkle carry her groceries. But even as Mrs. Periwinkle's cloud turned a soft pink in thanks, Elara's stayed the same dull grey. It only drizzled. She saw the village leader, Mr. Borum, whose cloud was always a brilliant yellow. Yet, Elara knew he was mean to his dog and cheated at cards. How could his cloud be so bright? This cloud business made no sense.

One afternoon, Elara saw an old woman sitting by the village well. Her face was wrinkled, her clothes were simple, but her eyes held a calm look. Above her head floated a cloud unlike any Elara had seen. It wasn’t bright yellow or angry black. It was a soft, misty white, almost like a thin morning fog. It seemed very peaceful.

Elara walked closer. "Excuse me," she said. "Your cloud is very different. How do you keep it so… calm?"

The old woman looked up, her smile gentle. "Oh, child. You worry about your cloud too much, I think."

"But mine is always grey," Elara said, a sigh escaping her lips. "It makes people think I'm sad or grumpy. I try to be happy, but it doesn't change."

The old woman chuckled softly. "The clouds don't care about your happy face, dear. Or your sad one. They listen to something else. They listen to what's true in your heart, not what you show on the outside."

"What do you mean?" Elara asked, confused.

"They listen to kindness," the old woman said, looking into Elara’s eyes. "A truly kind thought makes it bright. An unkind one makes it dark. The grey, drizzly ones... those are for those who are confused about kindness, or for those who try to force it for themselves."

Elara thought about Mr. Borum, the mean leader with his bright cloud. She thought about her own attempts to be happy just so her cloud would change. She had been thinking about herself, not about kindness itself.

Just then, a small boy, running too fast, tripped on a loose stone. He fell with a loud cry, scraping his knee on the rough path. Tears welled up in his eyes. Without thinking, Elara dropped her basket and ran to him. She knelt down, her own knee brushing the dusty ground. "Oh, you poor thing," she said, her voice soft and full of real concern. She gently helped him sit up, wiping his tears with her sleeve. She didn't think about her own cloud, or how it looked. She just wanted the boy to feel better. "It's just a little scrape," she soothed. "Let's get you to your mother, and we'll put some cool water on it." She helped him stand, keeping a steadying arm around him.

### Conclusion
As Elara walked the boy home, holding his hand, a strange warmth spread through her. Above her head, her dull, grey cloud began to shift. The drizzle stopped. Patches of soft yellow began to show through the grey, then widened, mixing into a gentle, hopeful orange. It wasn't the brilliant yellow of Mr. Borum's cloud, but it was warm, real, and clear. Elara finally understood: the clouds weren't about your feelings; they were about your honest goodness, shining out for the world to see.
