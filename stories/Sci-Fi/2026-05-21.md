# The Orange Flicker

**Genre:** Sci-Fi | **Date:** 2026-05-21

### Characters
Kael: A diligent night technician, observant and solitary.
Mira: Kael's supervisor, practical and focused on established protocols.

### The Story
Kael loved the quiet hum of the Core Chamber. It wasn't really a chamber, more like a cavern, deep under the city. Cold air filled the space, smelling faintly of ozone and old dust. In the very middle, a single, vast pillar rose from the floor and met the ceiling. On its surface, about halfway up, was the Core Light. It pulsed a steady, soft green. Always green.

Kael’s job was simple: sit, watch, log. He monitored the Core Light for any changes. Its brightness, its rhythm. If it ever shifted from green to amber, he’d note it down. Red meant an emergency, though no one had seen red in decades. The Core Light, they said, powered the entire city of Veridia above. It was the heart.

Every night, Kael would bring his flask of lukewarm nutrient paste, his small log pad, and settle into his chair. He’d stare at the green glow. It was boring, yes, but peaceful. He liked the peace. Most of Veridia was bright, loud, and always moving. Down here, it was just him and the green light.

One Tuesday night, while Kael was halfway through his paste, it happened. The Core Light flickered. For less than a second, the steady green *burst* into a brilliant, vibrant orange. Then, just as suddenly, it snapped back to green. Kael blinked. He checked his log pad. No anomaly registered. No tremor, no power spike. Nothing.

He leaned forward, heart thumping. Had he imagined it? No, it had been too clear. He typed a note into his personal log: "03:17 – Core Light observed brief, intense orange flicker. Unscheduled. Duration < 1s. No system error detected." He sent a quick message to Supervisor Mira. Her reply came back quickly: "Probably a retinal burn from staring too long, Kael. Check your diagnostic scans. All clear here. Stick to protocol."

Kael sighed. He knew she would say that. He went back to watching the green.

The next night, it happened again. 01:45. A flash of orange, quicker this time. And with it, Kael felt a strange warmth spread through him, not from the light itself, but somehow *inside* him. He also thought he smelled something – sweet, like blossoms after rain. But that was impossible, down here.

He logged it. He didn't bother Mira this time. She’d just tell him to get more sleep.

Over the next few weeks, the orange flickers became more frequent. Not daily, but often enough for Kael to start anticipating them. Each time, the warmth, the faint smell, the feeling of something vast and ancient stirring. He started to feel a pull, a strange sense of… recognition. As if the orange light wasn't just a colour, but a greeting.

He read old files, dusty theories about the Core Light. Most said it was a quantum energy reactor. Some even suggested it was a captured star, harnessed for power. But none mentioned orange. None mentioned feelings, or smells.

One night, the orange light lingered for nearly five seconds. As it glowed, Kael felt an overwhelming sense of wonder, a quiet voice speaking not in words, but in pure, clear feeling. *Hello. We see you.*

He stumbled back, hitting his chair. This wasn't a reactor. This wasn't a power source. This wasn't even a light.

### Conclusion
Kael raced to the old, forbidden section of the chamber, a small, sealed-off door he'd never opened. He forced it open, the old metal groaning. Inside, he found a terminal with ancient schematics. The 'Core Chamber' wasn't a power plant. It was an optical array, a massive, deep-space telescope pointed through the planet itself. The 'Core Light' was not a city power source, but a *distant, small star*, thousands of light-years away, its faint light collected and projected onto the pillar for observation. The city, in its rush for advancement, had long forgotten its true purpose, believing it merely drew power from the "light." Kael realized then: the orange flickers, the warmth, the scent, the feeling of vastness, the quiet 'Hello' – they weren't anomalies. They were *messages*. Someone out there was changing their star's output, a cosmic beacon, and Kael, alone in the silent, cold chamber, was the first human in centuries to truly see, feel, and understand the greeting from across the void. The city wasn't *using* the Core Light; it was being watched *by* it. Or rather, by those who now spoke through it.
