# The Echo-Glove's Secret

**Genre:** Sci-Fi | **Date:** 2026-05-07

### Characters
Leo: A quiet, imaginative man, our main character.
Mira: Leo's friendly neighbor, pragmatic and cheerful.

### The Story
Leo often felt like a ghost in his own life. Days passed in a blur of routine: work, eat, sleep. He dreamed of grand adventures, of touching history, but his small apartment offered only quiet. Then he saw an ad for the "Echo-Glove." It promised to let you "feel the past" by simply touching an object. He ordered one right away.

The Echo-Glove arrived in a slim, plain box. It was a simple, grey glove, smooth and light. Leo slipped it on. The instructions were easy: "Touch an object. Focus. Feel." He found an old teacup in his cupboard, chipped and forgotten. He pressed the gloved palm against its cool surface.

A warmth spread through his hand. Then, a rush of senses. He felt delicate, long-gone hands gently lift the cup. He heard faint, polite laughter and the soft clink of spoons. He smelled a light, sweet scent, like jasmine tea. The experience lasted only a few seconds, but it was incredibly real, vivid. He pulled his hand away, breathing fast. He had truly been there, in a moment from the past. It was amazing.

From that day, Leo became a collector of old things. He scoured thrift shops and antique markets. He bought a worn leather-bound book, and through the glove, he felt the rough parchment, smelled faint ink, and heard the whisper of turning pages in a quiet library. He bought a dusty, forgotten violin, and touching it, he felt smooth, old wood, smelled faint rosin, and heard distant, yearning melodies. He even found a rusted old key, which let him feel cold, worn metal, hear the jingle of other keys, and a distant, urgent clanking sound, like a gate being opened. Each experience was a tiny, perfect adventure.

He stopped going out much. His neighbor, Mira, would sometimes knock, inviting him for coffee or a walk in the park. "Come on, Leo," she'd say through the door. "Live a little!" But Leo felt like he *was* living. He was experiencing a thousand lives, a thousand histories, all from the comfort of his small apartment. His own life felt dull in comparison.

One Saturday, he found a real treasure: a small, intricately carved wooden box at a dusty antique shop. The shop owner claimed it was very old, maybe hundreds of years. This, Leo thought, would be his grandest journey yet. He paid for it with trembling hands and rushed home.

He set the box on his table, his heart thumping. He carefully put on the Echo-Glove, then slowly, reverently, pressed his hand against the ancient wood. A tidal wave of sensations hit him. He felt strong, skilled hands, firm but gentle, carefully shaping the wood. He heard the rhythmic scraping of a chisel, smelled fresh wood shavings and beeswax. He saw complex patterns emerging under his touch. It was overwhelmingly real, a deep dive into the work of a master craftsman long dead. The sheer focus, the dedication, the quiet joy of creation – Leo felt it all.

As the intense feeling began to fade, a small, odd detail snagged his attention. A faint, metallic smell, almost like warm circuits, drifted through the historical scent of wood and beeswax. It was familiar, somehow. And the feeling in the hands he had just experienced – that faint, repetitive ache in the knuckles – it was also familiar. He’d felt it before, in many of his "past" experiences through the glove.

A cold, strange thought popped into his head. He quickly pulled off the Echo-Glove. He looked at the carved box, then at his hand, then at the glove itself. He picked up the first everyday object he saw from his *own* apartment: his toothbrush. He placed it carefully on the table.

Then, with a growing sense of dread, Leo slipped the Echo-Glove back on. He didn't touch the carved box again. Instead, he slowly, deliberately, pressed his gloved hand against his own toothbrush.

### Conclusion
He expected to feel the gritty bristles, the faint mint of toothpaste, the feel of his own mouth from this morning. Instead, he felt the same familiar aches in the knuckles of his hand. He smelled that same faint, warm metallic scent, like circuits humming. He heard a soft, distant hum that he now realised was the subtle whir of the Echo-Glove itself, playing in the background of every 'historical' experience. It wasn't the toothbrush's past he was feeling. It wasn't the teacup's past. Or the book's. Or the violin's. The Echo-Glove wasn't playing back the history of the objects he touched. It was playing back *his own* sensory memories of *using the Echo-Glove* on those very objects. He had been reliving his *own* journey of discovery, the thrill of *his own* addiction, looped and projected onto each new item he held, always from *his own* perspective. The 'other lives' had been nothing more than echoes of his own desire to escape, reflected back to him by the device he so eagerly embraced. He took the glove off. It was time, he knew, to finally live his own life, without echoes.
