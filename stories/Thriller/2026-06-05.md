# The Other Sarah's Notes

**Genre:** Thriller | **Date:** 2026-06-05

### Characters
*   Sarah Miller: A woman in her late twenties, living a quiet, orderly life.
*   Mrs. Henderson: An elderly resident in Sarah's apartment building, with a gentle but confused manner.

### The Story
The first note appeared on a Tuesday. Sarah Miller found it tucked under her car's windshield wiper blade. It was small, neatly typed, on thick cream paper. "That scarf suits you. Good choice." Sarah felt a small shiver, even though the morning was warm. She *had* worn her green scarf that day, the one she rarely wore. A coincidence, she told herself, a random compliment. But her heart gave a little thump.

A few days later, another note. This one was left on her doormat, held down by a small stone. "Your coffee shop order was late today. Hope it wasn't too much trouble." Sarah stared at it, her blood turning cold. Her coffee *had* been late. She’d even grumbled about it to herself. How could anyone know that? Panic began to prick at her. She started checking her locks twice, glancing over her shoulder on the street.

The notes kept coming. Tucked into her newspaper, left on the railing by her apartment door, even once wedged into a grocery bag she brought inside. "That report looked tricky, you handled it well." "Forgot your vitamins again." "Sleepless night?" They were always about her, always accurate, always too personal. Sarah felt a constant knot in her stomach. She changed her routines, took different routes to work, even bought a new, cheaper brand of coffee. Nothing helped. The notes adapted, describing her new habits with chilling precision. She told no one, afraid of sounding crazy, afraid of making it more real.

The fear built like a dam about to burst. She bought a basic security camera for her door, but it only showed empty hallways. She imagined eyes on her, always. She barely slept. Her quiet, orderly life had become a cage. She was convinced someone was inside her apartment, even though she found no proof. The notes *felt* like they'd been placed there.

Then came the final note. It wasn't typed; it was handwritten in a shaky, old-fashioned script, on the same cream paper. She found it stuck to her bedroom door with a piece of tape. It was longer, almost a letter. "I know you think I'm a monster, Sarah. But I'm just trying to help. You've been struggling. I know what you need. Meet me at the old pier at midnight. Don't tell anyone."

Sarah trembled, clutching the note. A monster? Was this person going to hurt her? But the urge to end this, to face her tormentor, was stronger than her fear. She had to know. She had to stop it.

### Conclusion
At exactly midnight, Sarah stood by the old pier. The air was cold, damp. A single, flickering lamp cast long shadows. Then she saw a figure, small and hunched, waiting by a broken bench. As she got closer, her heart hammered. It was Mrs. Henderson, her frail, elderly neighbor from two floors down. Mrs. Henderson turned, her face wrinkled but her eyes bright with a strange mix of relief and confusion.

"Oh, thank goodness, *you're here*," Mrs. Henderson said, her voice thin. She held out a crumpled piece of cream paper, identical to the one Sarah had just read. "I left this note for *my* Sarah. She's been so distant lately, you see. And you, dear, you're always so busy. I just wanted to make sure she was okay. She has the same green scarf as you, you know. And she works on those tricky reports too. And sometimes, she forgets her vitamins. I just want to help her. I thought you were her for a moment there, by the pier. But you're not. My Sarah is my daughter. She lives on the floor above you. My memory isn't what it used to be. But I remember trying to help my Sarah."

Sarah looked from the note in the old woman's hand to the one she still clutched, which clearly had "Sarah" written on it. Her name. Her own paranoia, coupled with a simple, sad confusion, had turned an old woman's muddled affection for her daughter into a terrifying ordeal. The notes were always meant for a *different* Sarah, a daughter the old woman worried about, whose life coincidentally mirrored hers in small, everyday ways. Mrs. Henderson merely picked up on details of Sarah Miller's life, thinking it was her own daughter. The "inside" notes? They were always left in common areas, on doormats, or tucked into mail that Sarah brought inside, her mind then convinced they appeared magically in her private space. The twist wasn't a monster or a grand conspiracy, but a heartbreaking mix of mistaken identity and a mother's fading memory, leaving Sarah with a chill that was both of relief and profound sadness.
