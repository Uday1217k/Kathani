# The Odd Package

**Genre:** Thriller | **Date:** 2026-06-19

### Characters
Alex: A delivery driver, about thirty years old.
Brenda: Alex's dispatcher, a busy woman.
Dr. Elena Rostova: A medical scientist.

### The Story
Alex pulled his delivery van into the company parking lot. It had been a long day, full of narrow streets and grumpy dogs. He just wanted to go home, eat, and sleep. He started pulling out the empty crates, ready for the next shift. As he cleared the back, his hand hit something solid under a pile of old packing peanuts. It was a package.

He stared at it. It was a plain brown box, no labels, no sticker, no writing. It wasn't on his delivery list. Alex felt a small thrumming coming from inside the box, like a phone on vibrate, but deeper. He picked it up. It was heavier than it looked, and felt slightly warm. A very faint, soft hum came from it.

"Brenda!" he called, carrying the box into the office. Brenda, his dispatcher, was already shutting down her computer. "Look at this. Found it in my van. No address, no list. Just... this."

Brenda glanced at the box, then at Alex’s tired face. "Leftover, probably. Someone dropped it, or it fell off a shelf at the depot. Just leave it. We'll sort it out tomorrow." She waved him off.

But Alex couldn't shake the feeling. The hum seemed to get louder when he held it. He put the box in his locker. He tried to forget it, but the hum felt like it vibrated in his chest even from inside the locker. He opened the locker door. The box was still humming. He reached for it, curiosity getting the better of him. He shook it gently. Nothing rattled. He held it to his ear. The hum was steady. He saw a tiny gap where the box wasn't fully sealed. He carefully peeled it open a little further. Inside, he saw a glimmer of polished silver, and a tiny, faint red light blinking. It looked like a small screen, showing a string of numbers.

His mind went to movies he'd seen. A bomb. A timer. The blood drained from his face. He quickly pushed the flap back down. This wasn't some lost parcel. This was something dangerous. He had to get it out of here.

He grabbed the box and walked fast out of the depot. He didn't want to leave it for Brenda or anyone else. He had to deal with it himself. He got into his own car, placing the box carefully on the passenger seat. He drove aimlessly, trying to think. Where could he take it? A police station? No, they'd ask questions. And what if it really was a bomb? He couldn't risk anyone.

His phone rang. It was an unknown number. He almost didn't answer. He did. A low, calm voice said, "Are you the one with the package? The one from Swift Parcels?"

Alex gripped the steering wheel. "Who is this?" he asked, his voice shaking a little.

"You have something that isn't yours," the voice said. "It's vital. Do not open it. Do not interfere with it. Just drop it."

"Drop it where?" Alex asked, his heart thumping. "Is this a bomb?"

The voice sighed, a frustrated sound. "It's not a bomb. It's a prototype. Extremely sensitive. Deliver it to the coordinates. You have them on your updated manifest, driver. Check your work phone." The line went dead.

Alex fumbled for his work phone, which he'd left on the charger in his van. He ran back, grabbed it, and looked. There it was. A new entry. A special delivery, added late. A remote address, very specific coordinates. And the name of the sender was a medical research company. The recipient was 'Dr. Elena Rostova.'

He looked from the address on the phone to the humming box. A prototype. Not a bomb. But still, the warning in the voice made him uneasy. He drove. The coordinates took him far out of the city, down winding country roads, to a cluster of buildings that looked like a private lab. A black car, sleek and silent, pulled up behind him just as he stopped. Two men in dark suits got out.

Alex grabbed the box, heart racing again. "It's not what you think!" he yelled, holding the box out in front of him like a shield.

One of the men held up a hand. "Sir, calm down. Please just deliver the package. Dr. Rostova is waiting."

Alex walked into the main building. A woman with sharp eyes and a white lab coat rushed towards him. "You're Alex? Thank goodness! The manifest update was delayed. Did you open it?" she asked, her voice urgent.

"Just a little," Alex mumbled, handing her the box. "I saw numbers. A red light. I thought..."

### Conclusion
Dr. Rostova carefully took the box. She held it close, relief washing over her face. "The numbers? That was the remote patient monitoring system! My experimental vital sign tracker. We were about to lose a critical data stream. The red light just shows it's active. It's not a timer, Alex. It's a lifesaver. You were supposed to drop it at the hospital wing first, then here for calibration. But you grabbed it before the first location was added to your device. We've been trying to track it for hours." Alex watched her hurry away, the "bomb" now a humming box of hope, and he realized his biggest fear had been nothing more than a simple delivery mix-up.
